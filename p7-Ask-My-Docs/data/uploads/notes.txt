3. Suggestions: What to invest in Nigeria, and how to reduce risks

Given the local environment (inflation, currency risk, fraud risk, regulatory risk), here are some strategies / instruments and what to watch out for.

What to consider

Diversification: don’t put all money in one type. Spread across assets, sectors, maybe currencies.

Hedge against inflation: Try to find investments that at least outpace inflation, or expose you to assets whose value tends to grow in inflationary periods (real estate, foreign-denominated assets, etc.).

Lower overhead / avoid high fees: Fees erode returns, especially when margins are thin. Look carefully at commissions, foreign exchange spreads, withdrawal fees.

Regulation & verification: Use platforms licensed by SEC Nigeria; check real estate titles, brokers. Be wary of schemes promising very high returns with low risk.

Currency risk: Because Naira often depreciates, if you can get exposure to foreign currency (dollars, euros) or foreign assets, that helps preserve value.

What I’d put my money into (if I were you) in Nigeria

Here are realistic and relatively safe / moderate-risk investment options you could explore:

Government bonds / savings bonds / treasury bills

Use the ones offered by the Nigerian government with reasonable yields.

If possible, some in foreign currency (e.g. Eurobonds), depending on your risk tolerance and access.

Local blue-chip stocks with good dividend histories

Pick companies with stable earnings, good governance, strong local demand.

You might not get huge growth, but dividends + moderate capital growth.

Consider via trusted brokers/apps.

Mutual funds / AMCs

Especially funds that invest in a mix: some stocks, some treasury bills, maybe foreign assets.

This reduces risk from any single investment.

REITs / Real Estate

Either buy property if you can, or invest in REITs if available.

Choose location carefully (some cities grow faster). Legal title is critical.

Foreign exposure

Through platforms like Bamboo, Trove, etc., to buy some US or global stocks or ETFs; this helps hedge against local currency devaluation. Put maybe a part of your portfolio here.

Fixed Income / Savings with good interest

Fixed Deposits or bank-instruments with decent interest. If inflation is large, you want interest + capital appreciation.

Small business / Private ventures

If you understand a business or sector well (e.g. agriculture, tech, services), putting capital into your own or trusted ventures can yield high returns. But high risk.

Agriculture / Commodities

Investing in farmland, agriculture, or commodities can be a hedge. Some platforms or cooperatives allow fractional involvement.

What to avoid / watch out for

Too-good-to-be-true schemes (high monthly returns with low risk)

Platforms without regulation/licence

Poor contract/legal documentation (especially in property)

High hidden fees (FX spreads, commissions, withdrawal fees)

Currency traps: gains might be good in Naira, but if Naira falls a lot, actual value in stable currency goes down.
